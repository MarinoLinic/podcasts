Summary:
Ryan Burge argues that the "Great Secularization" of America has hit a hard floor, entering a period of permanent stasis rather than a religious revival. He details the collapse of the Mainline Protestant church through the lens of his own congregation's closure, the rise of "Non-Denominational" evangelicalism as the dominant future force, and the misunderstood demographics of belief (where the wealthy and educated are actually the most religious). The discussion covers the "gender flip" in Gen Z, the failure of online church, the limits of immigrant religion, and the persistence of supernaturalism over intellectualized faith.

### **1. The "Nones" and the Plateau of Secularization**
*   **The "Dispensary vs. Church" Confusion:**
    *   The interview opens with an anecdote about driving past a factory-like building called "Ascend." It was unclear if it was a nondenominational church or a marijuana dispensary. It turned out to be weed. This illustrates the blurry aesthetic of modern American culture.
*   **Defining the "Nones":**
    *   The term refers to those who select "None of the above" on religious surveys. It includes three distinct subgroups:
        1.  **Atheists:** Explicit rejection of God.
        2.  **Agnostics:** Unsure/Unknowable.
        3.  **Nothing in Particular:** The largest and most overlooked group. These are not committed secularists or ideologues. They look at the menu of options (Catholic, Baptist, Muslim) and simply shrug. They are characterized by apathy rather than antipathy.
*   **The Statistical Trend:**
    *   **1972:** Nones were 5% of the US population.
    *   **Today:** Nones are ~30% of the population. This is the single largest demographic shift in modern American social history.
    *   **The Shift (1991–2020):** This was the era of rapid secularization.
    *   **The Plateau (2020–Present):** We have entered a period of stasis. For the last five years, Christian identification has held steady at ~63–65%, and Nones have stuck at ~30%.
*   **The "Bedrock" Theory:**
    *   Burge argues the rapid decline was the "scraping off of loose topsoil"—people who were culturally affiliated but rarely attended mass (perhaps once every few years).
    *   **The Cold War Factor:** In the 1950s–80s, atheism was stigmatized as un-American and associated with Communism. As that stigma evaporated post-Cold War, people felt free to admit they were non-religious.
    *   **Current State:** We have hit "bedrock." The remaining Christians are true believers or deeply integrated socially. It is unlikely non-religious numbers will rise significantly above 30% because a core religiosity remains unique to America compared to Western Europe.
*   **Belief vs. Belonging Hierarchy:**
    *   **Behavior (Attendance):** This drops first.
    *   **Belonging (Identity):** This drops second.
    *   **Belief:** This is the last to go. Even among "never-attenders," belief in God often remains higher than convinced atheism.
*   **The Vibe Shift:**
    *   The "New Atheist" movement (Hitchens, Dawkins) was culturally dominant 15–20 years ago. Today, they are seen as "tired" and minor players. The cultural "vibe" has shifted toward a reconsideration of religion's utility, even if attendance hasn't followed suit.

### **2. Demographics: Gender, Class, and Education**
*   **The Gender Reversal (Gen Z):**
    *   *Historical Norm:* Across almost all cultures (Christian, Muslim, etc.), women are more religious than men. Theories range from biological predispositions to sociological needs (safety nets for child-rearing).
    *   *Current Trend:* Young women (Gen Z) are leaving the church at a significantly faster rate than young men. Men are still leaving, but slowly.
    *   *The Convergence:* This is leading to a 50/50 split in pews (down from 75% female in some older cohorts).
    *   *Implication:* While potentially healthier for the "marriage market" within churches (more eligible men), it poses a long-term threat because mothers have historically been the primary transmitters of religious habit to children. If women leave, the intergenerational chain breaks.
*   **The Myth of the "Religious Poor":**
    *   **Europe vs. America:** In Europe, the elite are secular, and the poor/less educated are religious. In America, this is inverted.
    *   **The Modal Churchgoer:** The person most likely to attend church in the US has a bachelor’s degree, makes $60k–$100k a year, is married, and has children.
    *   **"Having Your Shit Together":** Religiosity in America is now a marker of social stability, institutional trust, and "polite society."
    *   **The "Dropout" Matrix:** Those with low education/income are "dropping out" of civil society entirely: they don't vote, they don't go to church, and they don't participate in the labor force.
*   **The "Woo" Fallacy (Spirituality):**
    *   There is a pervasive myth (visible on Barnes & Noble shelves filled with Tarot and crystals) that people are replacing religion with "spirituality."
    *   **Data Reality:** The most spiritual people are **religious people**. Those who leave religion generally replace it with *nothing*, not paganism.
    *   **The "Buffet Catholic":** Much of the interest in astrology/mysticism comes from people who are *also* attending Christian churches (syncretism), rather than purely secular neo-pagans. The "Witchcraft" demographic is loud on TikTok but statistically a "rounding error."

### **3. The Collapse of the Mainline & The Rise of "Non-Denom"**
*   **The Mainline Freefall:**
    *   **The Seven Sisters:** Methodists, Baptists (American), Lutherans, Presbyterians, Episcopalians, etc. These were once the pillars of American community.
    *   **The Decline:** In 1958, over 50% of Americans were on Mainline rolls. Today, it is ~8.5%. The average age in these denominations is near 60.
*   **Burge’s Personal Case Study:**
    *   Burge pastored the First Baptist Church of Mt. Vernon, Illinois, for 17 years.
    *   **The trajectory:** Founded in 1868. Built a 14,000 sq ft building on 7 acres in the 1960s for 300+ attendees.
    *   **The end:** By 2024, attendance was 10–12 people on a Sunday. They couldn't afford insurance or maintenance. They gave the building away to a private Christian school and dissolved the church.
    *   *Context:* Burge notes he was hired at 24 with no seminary training because of a "supply and demand" crisis in rural ministry—no one else wants the job.
*   **Demerath’s Theory of Success:**
    *   Sociologist J.P. Demerath argued the Mainline declined because it *succeeded*. It convinced American culture to adopt its values (pluralism, tolerance, free speech).
    *   **"Country Club with Jesus":** Once the culture became Mainline, the church lost its distinctiveness. People realized they could just go to a real country club without the sermons.
*   **The "Non-Denom" Future:**
    *   **The Growth:** Nondenominational Christianity grew from 3% (1972) to 15% (today). It is the second biggest religious story after the Nones.
    *   **Characteristics:**
        *   **Evangelical but Anti-Institutional:** No headquarters, no oversight. Money stays in the local building.
        *   **Fragmented:** "Fiefdoms" led by individual pastors (e.g., Joel Osteen). Franklin Graham and Paula White are both "Evangelical" but occupy totally different universes and would never interact.
        *   **"Christianity Lite":** They avoid strident dogmas to remain palatable. Sermons focus on self-help ("3 ways to be a better father") and soft-pedal hard stances on gay marriage in public, even if they remain conservative in private.
*   **Emotionalism vs. Intellect:**
    *   The Mainline failed because it was "all head, no heart" (overly intellectualized, dry).
    *   The growing sectors (Pentecostalism, Assemblies of God, Non-Denoms) prioritize **Supernaturalism and Emotion**. Drums, guitars, speaking in tongues, healing miracles, and long worship sets are the "winning" features of 21st-century religion.

### **4. Politics and Polarization**
*   **The Sorting:**
    *   Political ideology is now the #1 predictor of religiosity.
    *   **The Odds:** If you are a Liberal, there is a 50% chance you are non-religious. If Conservative, only a 12% chance.
    *   **Tribal Identity:** White Christianity is deeply coded as Republican. White Southern Baptists are 80% Republican.
*   **Bottom-Up Politicization:**
    *   Burge corrects the myth that pastors are preaching MAGA sermons. Most pastors avoid politics to keep the peace.
    *   **The Parking Lot Reality:** Politics comes from the *pews*. The congregation creates a political culture (talking about Trump/Biden before and after service) that alienates liberals, regardless of what the pastor says.
*   **The Mainline Mistake:**
    *   Liberal churches thought moving Left politically (affirming LGBTQ+, focusing on social justice) would save them by attracting young people.
    *   **The Result:** It failed. Young liberals simply become secular rather than joining liberal churches. They don't see the point of a "religious" version of their existing politics.
*   **The "Consistent Ethic of Life" Trap:**
    *   The Catholic Church tries to be holistic (anti-abortion, but also anti-death penalty and pro-immigrant).
    *   **Failure:** In a polarized binary system, this pleases no one. Liberals hate the abortion stance; Conservatives hate the immigration stance. There is no "market" for a consistent ethic.
*   **Social Contract Theory:**
    *   The Mainline used to be the "purple" space where the CEO and the union worker sat together. Its collapse means there is no longer a shared space for the Right and Left to humanize each other, accelerating polarization.

### **5. Future Projections (2050)**
*   **The Winners:**
    *   **Non-Denominational Evangelicals:** Will dominate the landscape due to adaptability and emotional resonance.
    *   **Small, High-Intensity Groups:** Groups like the **ACNA** (Anglican Church in North America) and **PCA** (Presbyterian Church in America) are growing. They combine evangelical zeal with high-church liturgy/intellectualism (e.g., Tim Keller's influence).
    *   **Trad Caths (Traditional Catholics):** A small but influential minority ("The Triad"). They reject Vatican II, preferring Latin Mass, head coverings, and the priest facing the altar.
    *   **Strategic Growth:** While Trad Caths won't be a majority, their high fertility (7+ kids) and intense commitment mean they may capture the *institutional core* of Catholicism as the "normie" Catholics drift away.
*   **The Losers:**
    *   **Mainline Denominationalism:** Will likely cease to exist as large national bodies. Mergers are probable ("The Seven Sisters" will become one or two survivors).
    *   **Non-Christian Immigrant Faiths:** Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism face a "fertility cliff." Their growth relies entirely on new immigration.
    *   **The Assimilation Problem:** Second-generation immigrants often leave the faith of their parents to assimilate. In places like suburban Dallas, upwardly mobile Hispanics may leave Catholic parishes for "cool" non-denominational megachurches to fit in with the elite.
*   **The Amish:**
    *   They will continue to grow due to high fertility, but Burge rejects the "exponential takeover" theory.
    *   **The Cohesion Limit:** As religious groups get massive, they lose internal cohesion (The "Mormon Problem"). Retention rates drop as the community becomes too large to police effectively.
*   **The "Thomas Jefferson" Error:**
    *   In the 1800s, Jefferson predicted every young man would die a Unitarian (rational faith). Instead, the US got the Great Awakening (emotional faith).
    *   **Lesson:** The future is almost always weirder and more supernatural than rationalists predict.
*   **Church Planting:**
    *   The Mainline (Episcopalians) is starting to copy the Evangelical strategy of "Church Planting"—giving a young, charismatic priest $500k to start a fresh community from scratch rather than trying to revive a dying congregation in an old building.
*   **Technology as a Solvent:**
    *   The Internet is destructive to faith because it exposes hypocrisy and creates caricatures of the "other" (Atheists think Christians hate everyone; Christians think Atheists are amoral).
    *   **The Failure of Online Church:** During COVID, 90% of churches streamed. It failed. Even young people (2/3 majority) prefer in-person. The "killer app" of religion is **physical presence**—singing songs and shaking hands in a room. Digital religion has no "stickiness."
